Why Password Strength Matters

A short guide to safer passwords and fewer account takeovers.

Weak passwords are the main way accounts get hacked

When you reuse a simple password or use something easy to guess, you're relying on luck. Hackers use automated tools that try millions of common passwords (like "password123" or "qwerty") and leaked credentials from old breaches. If your password is short, predictable, or reused on another site, one breach can unlock several of your accounts.

Strong passwords won't stop every attack, but they make brute-force and dictionary attacks impractical. They're your first line of defense for email, banking, and any service that holds your personal data.

What makes a password strong?

Strength comes from length, variety, and unpredictability:

  • Length. Aim for at least 12 characters for important accounts. Longer is better; each extra character makes guessing far harder.
  • Variety. Mix uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and symbols so the password isn't a single word or pattern.
  • Unpredictability. Avoid birthdays, names, pet names, and real words. Random characters or a passphrase of several random words are much stronger.

How to create and manage strong passwords

Use a different password for every important account. That way, if one site is breached, your other accounts stay safe. Remembering dozens of strong passwords is unrealistic, so use a password manager: it can generate and store strong, unique passwords and fill them in when you log in.

Another option is a passphrase: several random words strung together (e.g. "correct-horse-battery-staple"). Passphrases are easier to remember than random characters and can be very strong if the words are random and not predictable.

Turn on two-factor authentication (2FA) wherever it's offered. Even if someone gets your password, they still need a second factor (like a code on your phone) to get in.
